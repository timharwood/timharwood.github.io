  • IA

52 words 1 min1 min read

The Au­di­ences por­tal pro­vides the BBC with in­for­ma­tion and in­sight around their au­di­ence and the con­sump­tion of their con­tent, via data such as view­ing / lis­ten­ing ﬁg­ures, de­mo­graph­ics, di­rect au­di­ence feed­back, and nu­mer­ous re­search pieces. It’s used through­out the or­gan­i­sa­tion, from the Di­rec­tor Gen­eral through to some­one mak­ing a lo­cal ra­dio ad.

I helped the BBC re­de­ﬁne the strat­egy for the por­tal, and then re­designed the so­lu­tion from the ground up.

Meet­ing the Client / Sell­ing the work

This pro­ject came through sup­port­ing the ex­ist­ing so­lu­tion. Pic­ture / Soft­wire won a BBC ten­der to sup­port the ex­ist­ing so­lu­tion, in YYYY, and es­tab­lished a work­ing re­la­tion­ship (es­pe­cially when the ex­ist­ing so­lu­tion im­ploded and we did a sig­nif­i­cant amount of work to get them back up and run­ning).

Within the BBC, and specif­i­cally the au­di­ences dept, there was the feel­ing that the cur­rent por­tal was no longer ﬁt for pur­pose: it no longer rep­re­sented the busi­ness (be­ing al­most en­tirely TV fo­cused), and it was at an end of life stage tech­ni­cally.

We’d pro­posed two part ap­proach to re­place the por­tal, start­ing with a re­search and scop­ing phase, fol­lowed by a (sep­a­rately ten­dered) de­sign and build phase, and when I moved to Soft­wire, this pro­ject ‘came with me’.

Part 1

The team for this part of the pro­ject com­prised of my­self and Si­mon West (who had been a reg­u­lar col­lab­o­ra­tor at Pic­ture), with some PM sup­port from Tom Ri­ley and tech sup­port from Us­man Iqbal.

Whilst the sec­tions be­low sug­gest a clear se­quen­tial­ity, in re­al­ity, lots of the ac­tiv­ity was con­cur­rent and/​or it­er­a­tive, where learn­ings or ideas would bounce be­tween ‘ar­eas’ to af­fect the whole (I think this is true of most pro­jects but is all but im­pos­si­ble to recre­ate in a case study).

Dis­cov­er­ing / Set­ting Goals

To get a bet­ter view of how the new por­tal would be a suc­cess, we un­der­took a num­ber of ac­tiv­i­ties with stake­hold­ers, au­di­ences team mem­bers, and users.

We or­gan­ised one-to-ones with the se­nior team, who gave us nu­mer­ous bits of in­for­ma­tion and tar­gets. The PO, Pete — who, with 20 years of ex­pe­ri­ence in the de­part­ment, had a vast amount of knowl­edge across the do­main — helped us start to pick apart the info we were gath­er­ing, and un­der­stand what of the sys­tem was use­ful, and why cer­tain things be­haved as they did.

With Pe­te’s help we wrote an on­line sur­vey, which was launched from the site, which al­lowed us to can­vas wide (but shal­low) opin­ion, but al­lowed us to add some depth to the an­a­lyt­ics we had, and get a more de­tailed view of who our users were, and some very top-line feed­back on the cur­rent sys­tem: what as­pects they val­ued (or missed) from; what tasks they were at­tempt­ing; what was tricky or well served. As ever we faced the chal­lenge of get­ting as much valu­able in­for­ma­tion whilst keep­ing the num­ber of ques­tions to a min­i­mum.

I lead a work­shop with a large cross sec­tion of the au­di­ences team, from roles across the BBC. We col­lab­o­rated on tasks in­clud­ing look­ing at strengths and weak­nesses of the cur­rent sys­tem (a fairly stan­dard swot); get­ting their de­f­i­n­i­tions of who users were and their goals (us­ing an adap­ta­tion of Leah Bu­ley’s el­e­va­tor pitches); and even get­ting them to help us break down con­tent as­pects, by cut­ting up UI print­outs and an­no­tate / group them (akin a UI au­dit a la Brad Frost, but with more fo­cus on the pur­pose of el­e­ments).
