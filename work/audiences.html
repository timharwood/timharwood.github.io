Com­pris­ing data such as view­ing / lis­ten­ing ﬁg­ures, de­mo­graph­ics, di­rect au­di­ence feed­back, and link­ing nu­mer­ous re­search pieces, it’s used through­out the or­gan­i­sa­tion, from the Di­rec­tor Gen­eral through to some­one mak­ing a lo­cal ra­dio ad.

Meet­ing the client / Sell­ing the work

It started in 2013 when Pic­ture (in part­ner­ship with Soft­wire) won a ten­der to sup­port the ex­ist­ing au­di­ence’s por­tal. Over the course of sev­eral months, we es­tab­lished a good work­ing re­la­tion­ship (es­pe­cially when the ex­ist­ing so­lu­tion im­ploded and we did a sig­nif­i­cant amount of work to get them back up and run­ning).

Within the BBC, there was the feel­ing that the cur­rent por­tal was no longer ﬁt for pur­pose: it no longer rep­re­sented the busi­ness (be­ing al­most en­tirely TV fo­cused), as well as be­ing at an end-of-life stage tech­ni­cally (as demon­strated by its fail­ure).

We pro­posed a two-part ap­proach to re­place the por­tal, start­ing with a re­search and scop­ing phase, fol­lowed by a de­sign and build phase — when I moved to Soft­wire, this pro­ject ‘came with me’.

Part 1 — Re­search & scop­ing

The team for this part of the pro­ject pri­mar­ily com­prised of my­self and Si­mon West (who had been a reg­u­lar col­lab­o­ra­tor at Pic­ture), with PM sup­port from Tom Ri­ley and tech sup­port from Us­man Iqbal.

Whilst the sec­tions be­low sug­gest a clear se­quen­tial­ity, in re­al­ity, lots of the ac­tiv­ity was con­cur­rent and/​or it­er­a­tive, where learn­ings or ideas would bounce be­tween ‘ar­eas’ to af­fect the whole. I think this is true of most pro­jects but it’s hard to recre­ate in a co­her­ent man­ner.

Ini­tial ground­work

To get a bet­ter un­der­stand­ing of what would make the new por­tal a suc­cess, we un­der­took a num­ber of ac­tiv­i­ties with stake­hold­ers, au­di­ences team mem­bers, and users.

We had one-to-ones with the se­nior team, who gave us nu­mer­ous bits of in­for­ma­tion and tar­gets. The Pro­ject Owner, Pete — who, with 20 year’s of ex­pe­ri­ence in the de­part­ment, had a vast amount of knowl­edge across the do­main — helped us start to pick apart the info we were gath­er­ing, and un­der­stand what of the sys­tem was use­ful, and why cer­tain things be­haved as they did.

We wrote a sur­vey, which al­lowed us to gather a wide (but shal­low) overview of ex­ist­ing por­tal users, adding some depth to the an­a­lyt­ics we had — get­ting a more de­tailed view of what as­pects they val­ued (or missed), what tasks they were at­tempt­ing, what was tricky or well served. As ever we faced the chal­lenge of get­ting as much valu­able in­for­ma­tion whilst keep­ing the num­ber of ques­tions to a min­i­mum.

We ran a work­shop with a cross-sec­tion of the au­di­ences team, from across the BBC, where we col­lab­o­rated on var­i­ous tasks: look­ing at cur­rent strengths and weak­nesses of the cur­rent sys­tem, gath­er­ing de­f­i­n­i­tions of who users / goals (adapt­ing Leah Bu­ley’s el­e­va­tor pitches) and even get­ting them to con­duct a UI au­dit (a la Brad Frost, but with more fo­cus on the pur­pose of el­e­ments).

At this point, all of our ac­tiv­i­ties were aimed more at widen­ing our un­der­stand­ing (and be­ing able to ask bet­ter ques­tions) than com­ing to any sort of con­clu­sion with re­gard to suc­cess­ful out­comes.

In­ves­ti­gat­ing the cur­rent sys­tem

Along­side our time with pro­ject stake­hold­ers, we spent some time with the ex­ist­ing sys­tem, at­tempt­ing to un­der­stand what processes it was en­abling and why.

Be­tween us, we re­viewed every nook & cranny, cap­tur­ing and group­ing con­tent types and func­tions; get­ting an overview of ﬂow and us­age, iden­ti­fy­ing pat­terns in the con­tent and (ap­par­ent) ar­eas of util­ity or mis­match.

I be­gan the process of doc­u­ment­ing the ar­chi­tec­ture and func­tional struc­ture, while Si­mon un­der­took an in-depth con­tent and an­a­lyt­ics au­dit. We com­bined this work with the in­put from stake­hold­ers to feed our strat­egy and to pro­vide fod­der for our user in­ter­view script.

Pri­mary User Re­search

Fol­low­ing on from the work­shops, we un­der­took a num­ber of one to one in­ter­views (circa 20, last­ing around an hour each) with nu­mer­ous users of the por­tal from across the BBC, trav­el­ling to far-ﬂung reaches (well Bris­tol & Man­ches­ter) and speak­ing to peo­ple from a va­ri­ety of roles and func­tions.

As ever, the aim was to get at the real user needs which our prod­uct should ad­dress (es­pe­cially the as­pects of the ‘new BBC ’ — the less TV-re­lated ones), and to get, as far a pos­si­ble, in­sights in to the way they ‘pic­ture the world’ (what terms they use, how they see the processes they un­der­take etc).

These in­ter­views took a form which is fairly com­mon in my ap­proach to these things: Si­mon and I de­vel­oped a script (used a guide for a free ﬂow­ing con­ver­sa­tion), which lead us across a num­ber of ar­eas, from gath­er­ing some per­sonal info (par­tic­u­larly around tech use — to al­low us to quan­tify other an­swers), through gen­eral in­for­ma­tion around their jobs (the tasks that make up their days, the reg­u­lar ac­tiv­ity), and on to their use of the por­tal (fo­cus­ing on *ac­tual* ac­tiv­ity — when they last logged in, what they did, times when they had spe­ciﬁc dif­ﬁ­cul­ties).

We col­lated our notes from these ses­sions (we did­n’t record and get tran­scripts for these in­ter­views, some­thing I’m now very keen on), and combed them for com­mon terms and ac­tiv­ity. We then used the pat­terns we found within these to cre­ate some out­line per­sona, and to be­gin to spec­u­late some user-jour­neys.

Mod­el­ling the do­main and data

We spent quite a while on the map­ping ex­pe­ri­ence as­pi­ra­tions, us­ing the goals and tasks we de­rived from our re­search, work­ing on an un­der­stand­ing the sorts of con­trols and con­tent re­quired.

A large dri­ver to this pro­ject was the change in the na­ture of the busi­ness and re­sult­ing data, and the de­sire to both bet­ter rep­re­sent this change, and to set a ﬂex­i­bil­ity for fu­ture changes.

We it­er­ated on the na­ture of the data and worked on a model that let us be ﬂex­i­ble and de­scribe all sorts of in­ter­ac­tions, set­tling on a com­plex, ab­stracted model, equally ca­pa­ble of rep­re­sent­ing TV pro­grammes, app down­loads, or pur­chases of Dr Who mem­o­ra­bilia.

Con­tent Strat­egy & IA

There was (and con­tin­ues to be) a chal­lenge in the in­for­ma­tion ar­chi­tec­ture, where there are sev­eral over­lap­ping/​com­pet­ing ‘ar­eas of con­cern’, and where these are com­pli­cated or­gan­i­sa­tion­ally.

From the re­search and mod­el­ling work, we sug­gested a num­ber of con­tent, func­tional and or­gan­i­sa­tional re­quire­ments to best meet the user needs.

We cre­ated sev­eral tax­onomies and nav­i­ga­tional struc­tures – based on ex­ist­ing BBC or­gan­i­sa­tions (such as iplayer and news), on or­gan­i­sa­tional mod­els and on the evolv­ing data-struc­ture — in or­der to test, and bet­ter un­der­stand the most ap­plic­a­ble com­bi­na­tion

Pre­sen­ta­tion & De­liv­ery

Through­out the scop­ing process we fed back reg­u­larly to the au­di­ences team, meet­ing at least once a week, to re­view the work to date, and the for us to im­part new learn­ings and gain in­sight from the do­main ex­perts (es­pe­cially Pete the PO).

We pro­duced ‘some’ doc­u­men­ta­tion (mainly in the form of Google docs and di­a­grams), but tried to keep it light to min­imise on cost im­pli­ca­tions.

At the end of the process, I did a num­ber of pre­sen­ta­tions, to sell the re­build pro­ject to the wider team and se­nior man­age­ment — pro­vid­ing the BBC with a deck, and sup­port­ing doc­u­men­ta­tion of our de­ci­sions.
