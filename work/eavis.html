Meet­ing the Client / Sell­ing the work

This was very much an op­por­tu­nity that evolved through con­ver­sa­tion, rather than as the re­sult of a pitch or fo­cused sell.

Soft­wire orig­i­nally worked with Red­burn through 2015 to de­velop red­burn­to­day as an in­ter­nal por­tal, pro­vid­ing their staff with up to the minute in­for­ma­tion. Al­though there was al­ways a glance to­wards this be­com­ing a client fac­ing tool.

The de­sign for this por­tal was un­der­taken by Af­ter the Flood, who pro­duced a hugely com­pre­hen­sive spec for the por­tal.

My in­volve­ment dur­ing this phase was ‘cer­e­mo­ni­al’ – the head of de­sign be­ing in the meet­ing pro­vid­ing a “we’re tak­ing this se­ri­ously” mes­sage. Later in this pro­ject, fol­low­ing var­i­ous con­ver­sa­tions with the client, this role mor­phed some­what into a de­sign QA one, where I spent some time with the dev team in­ter­ro­gat­ing the de­signs, un­der­stand­ing and trans­lat­ing some of the ﬁner points, and help­ing work out what ques­tions needed clar­i­ﬁ­ca­tion.

As time pro­gressed this ex­panded fur­ther to tackle some de­sign is­sues (such as tack­ling dif­ﬁ­cult SVG icons, or de­sign­ing for­got­ten CMS screens); such that, when there were post-launch is­sues with the de­sign, it be­came the job of my team to help tackle them.

Dis­cov­er­ing Goals

On launch, it quickly be­came ap­par­ent that their we all sorts of as­pects that did­n’t quite meet the user’s needs or ex­pec­ta­tions, and so I be­came in­volved in con­ver­sa­tions as to the best way to tackle these.

There were nu­mer­ous as­pects that I felt needed tack­ling – there was an un­der­ly­ing com­plex­ity to the IA that felt com­plex­ity wrong, al­though I lacked enough knowl­edge to be sure; and vi­su­ally every­thing was very com­pet­i­tive.

I pro­vided feed­back and some light-touch ap­proach sug­ges­tions as to how to tackle these; how­ever the feel from Red­burn was that the biggest is­sue was with ‘pub­li­ca­tions’ pages, es­pe­cially the or­der­ing / ﬁl­ter­ing.

Ini­tially, this seemed to be a ‘me­chan­i­cal’ thing, and I worked, with my team on in­ter­ac­tion de­sign im­prove­ments, par­tic­u­larly to ﬁl­ter­ing; how­ever it be­came ap­par­ent by workign on these, that the gen­eral con­cept for these pages was ‘off’, so we took a sev­eral steps back, to bet­ter un­der­stand the un­der­ly­ing use-cases for these.

Re­search

We un­der­took sev­eral work­shops with in­ter­nal groups, mixed across sales, re­search and ad­min within Red­burn, where we worked with them to hone down what they felt was core value of Red­burn to its cus­tomers; and by col­lab­o­ra­tively scor­ing, group­ing and ex­pand­ing on these with par­tic­u­lar fea­tures, we came at a dif­fer­ent pri­ori­ti­sa­tion for the por­tal.

It was rel­a­tively hard work, the par­tic­i­pants weren’t nec­es­sar­ily the most en­gaged with this sort of ap­proach, and were gen­er­ally quite dis­mis­sive, yet the work­shops ger­mi­nated very valu­able con­ver­sa­tions, which we able to cap­ture and utilise.

It be­came ap­par­ent that the thing Red­burn was most val­ued for was its sin­gu­lar, high-qual­ity opin­ion, so we de­ter­mined to re­fo­cus the de­sign to re­ﬂect this — sim­pli­fy­ing a lot of the ‘data’ around the as­pects that ‘Red­burn say you should know’.

We were due to en­gage in fur­ther ses­sions with Red­burn cus­tomers (again a dif­ﬁ­cult thing to con­vince the wider group to let us un­der­take); when some un­re­lated se­cu­rity is­sues with the ex­ist­ing de­vel­op­ment team caused a hia­tus in our work. Un­for­tu­nately when we re­sumed work a few months later, we were un­able to re­visit this. While I don’t feel that our ap­proach would have al­tered sig­nif­i­cantly, I do think that we lacked in some ‘re­search clout’ to al­low us to ar­gue the case for some de­sign ap­proaches, and which has left the de­sign more open to ‘steer’ from se­nior in­ter­nal staff.

Pro­to­typ­ing / Ideation

Aong­side the work­shops we’d en­gaged in, I’d had sev­eral dis­cus­sions with the Red­burn team about a more it­er­a­tive, pro­to­typ­ing ap­proach to de­sign­ing the pub­li­ca­tions. As part of the ‘qual­ity of opin­ion’ po­si­tion we’d set upon, we talked about how to com­mu­ni­cate the qual­ity of the pub­li­ca­tions, some­thing that was felt lack­ing in the tab­u­lar lay­out of the ini­tial de­sign.

I felt some­thing more card-like might al­low that sort of qual­ity (re­ﬂect­ing bet­ter the printed out­put) whilst al­low­ing for some more in­ven­tive re­spon­sive de­sign, and so cre­ated a sim­ple HTML mockup, which was ex­tremely well re­ceived.

From this I it­er­ated, work­ing in con­trols for swap­ping the dis­play (from card grid to tab­u­lar); con­trols for pro­vid­ing sub­ject group­ings and ul­ti­mately build­ing up to vari­a­tions on search­ing.

Search gen­er­ally was deemed to be the big op­por­tu­nity for the site, so search for pub­li­ca­tions was both a mi­cro­cosm of this, and an op­por­tu­nity to start try­ing out some ap­proaches.

Through­out, with ac­cess to real users cir­cum­scribed by Red­burn, we’d been un­der­tak­ing ad hoc and guer­rilla test­ing of the in­ter­face, fo­cus­ing on con­trols and the me­chan­i­cal in­ter­ac­tions; how­ever once we be­gan to try and test the search, I felt we needed to cre­ate some­thing more re­al­is­tic, in or­der to re­ally un­der­stand whether the me­chan­ics *work*. In or­der to do this, I used some build tools, in­clud­ing faker and some data dumps from the ex­ist­ing site.

We adopted an auto-sug­gest and ‘fed­er­a­tion’ ap­proach to the search, look­ing at the way ‘types’ of data can help guide the user, pro­vid­ing ex­tra scent for their jour­neys

De­sign­ing / It­er­at­ing

Whilst tak­ing a pro­to­typ­ing ap­proach for the pub­li­ca­tions, I also pro­duced more ‘tra­di­tion­al’ de­sign, us­ing Adobe XD to cre­ate de­signs for pages and for com­po­nents, mov­ing where sen­si­ble be­tween the ap­proaches.

XD al­lowed me to con­cen­trate on the vi­sual craft of some as­pects in a more de­tail at speed, and look at de­vel­op­ing pat­terns that feel co­her­ent across the site.

Start­ing with some mo­bile views, and adopt­ing some of the group­ing as­pects from the pro­to­type, I be­gan to de­velop some pat­terns around con­trols / lay­out; with com­mon struc­tures to group­ings and to po­si­tion­ing of el­e­ments across type

I tried to re­ﬂect the na­ture of the in­for­ma­tion in its rep­re­sen­ta­tion – there is a ‘scale’ and a change in speed, that’s im­por­tant to rep­re­sent, so the item might be some­thing that’s taken months to re­search, and is a ‘re­al’ pub­li­ca­tion; some­times it might be an email. Both have their value, but the na­ture needs to be ap­par­ent (one of my is­sues with the orig­i­nal de­sign was the lack of vi­sual hi­er­ar­chy).

Pre­sen­ta­tion / Feed­back

Us­ing XD let us share the de­sign ‘as pro­to­type’ with the Red­burn team, along with the code pro­to­type (which we shared as a sta­tic drop­box ﬁle), and get feed­back ‘in pro­gress’ very eas­ily (and in a more ‘re­al’ man­ner).

There was­n’t a spe­ciﬁc ‘for­mal’ ap­proach to pre­sent­ing – the na­ture of the work meant that it was rare for a week to pass with­out me sit­ting in a room with at least some of the Red­burn team, which lead to a much more ﬂuid ac­cep­tance (al­though it must be said that it made ‘done’ more ﬂuid term too). Where there were gaps be­tween meet­ing, or where there was in­ter­nal pre­sen­ta­tion to prep for, Skype calls and screen shar­ing al­lowed us to col­lab­o­rate ef­fec­tively.

De­liv­ery / Im­ple­men­ta­tion

The na­ture of Soft­wire’s on-go­ing work with Red­burn, meant that I’d done a rea­son­able amount of de­sign and pro­to­type work, be­fore the dev team were able to move on to this phase of work. In some ways this was good, as there was co­her­ence to the de­sign by this point, and plenty of things to point at and help ex­plain ‘what & how’; how­ever there’s noth­ing like the rigour of de­vel­op­ment, and real use to test out the de­sign and ﬁnd the break­ing points, so I did a rea­son­able amount of it­er­at­ing and/​or back-ﬁll­ing.

In a per­fect world, once a gen­eral di­rec­tion has been set, the de­sign­ers and de­vel­op­ers would be able to pair to work through im­ple­men­ta­tion speciﬁcs – un­for­tu­nately, at this point my sched­ule meant that I was­n’t as avail­able, and so had to cre­ate more ex­am­ples to be worked from (this is one of the chal­lenges an agency faces in try­ing to max­imise use of it’s peo­ple’s time, whilst also de­sign­ing/​de­vel­op­ing in a ‘mod­ern’ way).

Be­ing able to hand over some mean­ing­ful code in the form of the pro­to­types helps in the pass­ing of de­sign in­tent, and it’s some­thing where I think sketch is clearly ahead of it’s de­sign tool ri­vals in terms of han­dover.

Im­pact

To date, we’ve not had chance to get any mean­ing­ful quant data – the prod­uct is only avail­able to a se­lect few of Red­burn’s clients.
