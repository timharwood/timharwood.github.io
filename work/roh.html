As one of the world’s great the­atres, the Royal Opera House en­riches peo­ple's lives through opera and bal­let. In ad­di­tion to per­form­ing to au­di­ences of 1.5m, they work with over 30,000 peo­ple a year through cre­ative learn­ing pro­jects.

In 2017, I led the de­sign of their Learn­ing Plat­form, which aims to en­gage with a more di­verse au­di­ence (in par­tic­u­lar teach­ers), for the pur­poses of learn­ing and dis­cov­ery.

Pro­ject Overview

A large part of the Royal Opera House’s mis­sion is to ex­pand the au­di­ence for opera and bal­let — be­yond ob­vi­ous com­mer­cial ben­e­ﬁt of this, it's an oblig­a­tion to give as many peo­ple as pos­si­ble the op­por­tu­nity to en­joy won­der­ful art-forms.

They have long en­gaged with young au­di­ences, through work­shops, mati­nees, via their space in Thur­rock, and par­tic­u­larly by reach­ing out to teach­ers. How­ever, un­til re­cently the dig­i­tal as­pect of this was frag­mented and un­fo­cused, and con­se­quently the reach and ef­fect was less than it could be.

In late 2016, the ROH be­gan to de­velop a plan for a more co­or­di­nated dig­i­tal of­fer, and through the sum­mer of 2017, we worked with them to make it a re­al­ity. To­gether, we de­vised an ar­chi­tec­ture and con­tent hi­er­ar­chy to sup­port the needs of their au­di­ence, ad­dress­ing is­sues of do­main fa­mil­iar­ity and ca­pa­bil­ity, along with dis­cov­ery and life-cy­cle.

We ex­panded on the newly de­vel­op­ing ROH vi­sual lan­guage to bet­ter ﬁt these needs, push­ing at the edges of the emerg­ing pat­terns to cre­ate pat­terns that met our needs, while at the same time (hope­fully) con­tribut­ing the qual­ity and ro­bust­ness of the over­all pat­tern li­brary.

Ul­ti­mately, this was de­liv­ered us­ing Word­press, ﬁt­ting with other ex­ist­ing ROH of­fer­ings, and giv­ing a sig­nif­i­cant fu­ture de­vel­oper base (should they ever de­sire). We cus­tomised the pub­lish­ing sys­tem to suit their spe­ciﬁc needs around en­tity re­la­tion­ships.

Through the life of the pro­ject, I was in­volved in a num­ber of as­pects, de­scribed in more de­tail be­low. Win­ning the pro­ject Deﬁn­ing pro­ject scope & suc­cess cri­te­ria Con­tent ap­proach & in­for­ma­tion ar­chi­tec­ture Vi­sual lan­guage and in­ter­ac­tion de­sign De­vel­op­ment

Win­ning (and plan­ning) the pro­ject

We had a re­la­tion­ship with the Royal Opera House CTO, Joe Mc­Fad­den, from his time at the NT, and in early 2017 they in­vited us to their ros­ter re­newal process. This con­sisted of a ‘beauty pa­rade’, where Joe, and Phil Latham (the RO­H ’s Head of Dig­i­tal De­vel­op­ment) spent a day with teams through­out Soft­wire to eval­u­ate our ca­pa­bil­i­ties and grill us about our ap­proach.

Hav­ing passed this ﬁrst step, we re­ceived nu­mer­ous RFP in­vites for a va­ri­ety of in­ter­est­ing pro­jects — un­for­tu­nately it was­n’t un­til the Learn­ing Plat­form RFP, that we had a team avail­able to tackle one.

The RFP pre­sented some­thing of a chal­lenge, in that the scope it sug­gested for the pro­ject was am­bigu­ous (and po­ten­tially quite large) — far from a prob­lem in it­self, but an is­sue when com­bined with the time­line and bud­get. Some of the as­pects, for ex­am­ple around au­di­ence ac­qui­si­tion, seemed vague and at worst un­achiev­able for the prod­uct we were be­ing asked to cre­ate; at the same time, the brief con­tained nu­mer­ous wire­frames, sug­gest­ing that so­lu­tions had al­ready been de­ter­mined. This left us ques­tion­ing ex­actly what was re­quired for the pro­ject.

The ROH were ex­tremely open to our ques­tions, and were can­did about the work they’d done so far, what they felt could be achieved and what their con­cerns we’re for the pro­ject — through sev­eral calls, and face-to-face ses­sions, Gareth and I mapped out our un­der­stand­ing of the pro­ject, and from that were able to put to­gether a time­line of ac­tiv­i­ties and as­so­ci­ated costs (dur­ing our pro­ject retro, we re­vis­ited this ini­tial plan, and dis­cov­ered we were out by 1 day).

I think it was these ses­sions, as much as our pro­posal, which won us the pro­ject.

There were still sev­eral chal­lenges to be ad­dressed: the time­frame for the pro­ject was ex­tremely short, but tied to the­atri­cal pro­duc­tions, and the school year; the bud­get was in­cred­i­bly tight, even once some of our scope con­cerns were ad­dressed; and the lack of time/​bud­get to work with pri­mary users gave us con­cerns over de­sign­ing / de­vel­op­ing a sys­tem with plans for sig­nif­i­cant on­go­ing en­hance­ment.

Be­cause of the tight timescales, the ROH were un­der­stand­ably ner­vous – we put to­gether a plan, in­clud­ing an in­tense ini­tial pe­riod of col­lab­o­ra­tive work, to pro­vide early vis­i­bil­ity to stake­hold­ers and al­low enough time to make changes. In pitch­ing we dis­cussed in depth how we’d ap­proached a sim­i­lar work for pre­vi­ous clients, as well as speciﬁcs of meet­ing their needs for as­sur­ance.

Deﬁn­ing pro­ject scope & suc­cess cri­te­ria

This could also be ti­tled: get­ting stake­hold­ers to agree scope & suc­cess cri­te­ria, as there were a num­ber of peo­ple from across the ROH with in­put into the pro­ject – this is a fairly com­mon sce­nario, but cre­ates a chal­lenge around un­der­stand­ing / meet­ing their needs and to gain­ing a con­sen­sus of di­rec­tion.

The ﬁrst thing we did was to con­duct a short work­shop to gather as much in­for­ma­tion as pos­si­ble from them re­gard­ing the re­quire­ments they felt would lead to a suc­cess­ful pro­ject (and im­por­tantly for them to hear one-an­other too). We then took this in­for­ma­tion and as part of our ini­tial ac­tiv­i­ties found ways to play it back, and ‘ques­tion’ them, so as to get a bet­ter steer on their mean­ing.

For ex­am­ple, one re­quire­ment was that the de­sign im­parted ‘ROH-ness’, quite a tricky / ephemeral tar­get; so I gath­ered to­gether nu­mer­ous ex­am­ples from ex­ist­ing ROH ma­te­r­ial, and from the wider cul­tural ecosys­tem into a mood-board, which we then used to get more di­rect feed­back to help us align be­hind some more con­crete vi­sual goals.

Do­ing this high­lighted some grey ar­eas in terms of re­spon­si­bil­ity — in a very im­age rich en­vi­ron­ment, the ‘read­ing’ of that im­agery is core to the user’s un­der­stand­ing of a sys­tem, and de­sign­ing the sys­tem was very much ‘the scope’.

But, the choos­ing (or com­mis­sion­ing) of im­agery was­n’t part of our scope. There was an in­her­ent ten­sion, and we never re­ally came to a hard rule around this; but we were able to have con­ver­sa­tions around ‘the job’ that im­ages did at dif­fer­ing lev­els and dis­cussed at length the im­ages al­ready in use that cre­ated a co­he­sive whole and a sen­si­ble jux­ta­po­si­tion with the main site (and also look at the way other or­gan­i­sa­tions had used a dif­fer­ent pac­ing in their im­agery, e.g. the Globe The­atre’s won­der­ful ‘back stage’ and ‘au­di­ence’ pics).

Through­out the de­sign process, I tried where pos­si­ble to choose suit­able im­agery from the RO­H ’s ex­ist­ing li­brary, then sub­se­quently, fur­ther im­ages were com­mis­sioned in or­der to ﬁt the sys­tem, and, in the time since launch more, won­der­fully rich im­agery has been added.

Con­tent ap­proach & in­for­ma­tion ar­chi­tec­ture

There was lit­tle time & no bud­get for di­rect re­search (be­yond a lit­tle light guer­rilla re­search with teacher friends) – we had to move quickly, and rely on work al­ready done by the ROH, and depth of the Pro­ject Owner, Mis­sy’s knowl­edge — there had been a sig­nif­i­cant in­vest­ment in re­search to this point, and she clearly knew a lot about the do­main, and also had a very re­al­is­tic view on what could/​could­n’t be part of the pro­ject and how as­pects of the over­all user needs could be ad­dressed sep­a­rately.

We also had the li­brary of ex­ist­ing lessons and pro­grammes that the ROH had de­vel­oped and de­liv­ered over re­cent years, which var­ied from those tar­geted at pre-school­ers, all the way to a-level stu­dents, where the lessons are highly com­plex and de­mand­ing. This va­ri­ety helped us to be­gin think­ing about lev­els of en­tity and their prop­er­ties.

In or­der to max­imise our com­mon lan­guage from the very start, I took quite a ‘pre­sump­tu­ous’ po­si­tion and cre­ated a do­main map, based on my un­der­stand­ing from the RFP and our ini­tial, pre-pitch con­ver­sa­tions. Al­though sev­eral as­pects needed re­vi­sion, and ul­ti­mately a cou­ple of the re­la­tion­ships mu­tated once we started de­sign­ing; one of the real ben­e­ﬁts of this was that it helped get the other team mem­bers ‘more up to speed’.

We started with a work­shop with our team plus Missy, where we re­viewed the per­sonas, cre­ated by the ROH, and dis­cussed their ex­pected jour­neys, the con­tent we could pro­duce that would help ad­dress their needs. Then, along with the evolv­ing do­main map we’d pre­vi­ously been de­vel­op­ing, we pro­duced rough, post-it note wire­frames.

These we’re in­ten­tion­ally lo-ﬁ, so that we could con­cen­trate on the over­all re­la­tion­ships, and swap / ex­per­i­ment with el­e­ments, al­though a great bonus of this ap­proach is the abil­ity to cap­ture some el­e­ments of in­ter­face where it’s of par­tic­u­lar value. (Ad­di­tion­ally, colour can be used mean­ing­fully – not some­thing we pushed par­tic­u­larly in this in­stance, but we have pre­vi­ously – see Eavis)

From this ses­sion, on a Fri­day af­ter­noon, we were able to quickly pull to­gether a ‘block-frame’ pro­to­type in In­Vi­sion, to then play­back to a wider set of ROH stake­hold­ers on Mon­day – giv­ing us a much bet­ter base for con­ver­sa­tions about re­la­tion­ships and the roles spe­ciﬁc pieces of con­tent could per­form.

Among our or­gan­is­ing prin­ci­ples, in or­der to best serve the needs of our au­di­ence, were a cur­ricu­lum based hi­er­ar­chy and a fo­cus on time and re­source com­mit­ments (in or­der to pass what Missy de­scribed as ‘the Sun­day night test’). In ad­di­tion, we planned in con­text jour­neys from ‘snack-able’ con­tent, such as 5 min ‘how tos’ to deeper, struc­tured con­tent, like les­son plans.

We de­vised a Pro­gramme > Course > Mod­ule > Unit struc­ture, to cre­ate a ﬂex­i­ble and ex­pand­able plat­form where learn­ing plans re­quir­ing dif­fer­ing lev­els of com­mit­ment and ca­pa­bil­ity could be cre­ated, based around par­tic­u­lar pro­duc­tions (which bring op­por­tu­ni­ties of visit and re­view) or equally around spe­ciﬁc ac­tiv­ity (which al­lows for spe­ciﬁc tai­lor­ing and pro­gres­sion)

Vi­sual lan­guage and in­ter­ac­tion de­sign

Prob­a­bly the trick­i­est as­pects of the pro­ject were align­ing the de­sign of the learn­ing plat­form, with the on­go­ing de­sign of the new ROH de­sign sys­tem, and with the thoughts of the in­ter­nal UX team. The de­sign sys­tem was still in an em­bry­onic stage, and took some per­suad­ing, and chas­ing, to gain ac­cess — for the most part we ‘dis­cov­ered’ pat­terns by de­sign­ing things, and then be­ing given the equiv­a­lent pat­tern that was in progress to match to.

In­evitably there was a ten­sion that hap­pened be­cause of the com­pet­ing ‘needs’ of the de­signs, the am­bi­tion of some of the stake­hold­ers, the slightly pro­saic ap­proach of the in­ter­nal team, and the lack of clar­ity of emerg­ing ap­proach.

We ad­dressed this through a com­bi­na­tion of things: try­ing to show work early and of­ten, as a way of gaug­ing re­ac­tion, and to check we weren’t veer­ing away from the de­sign sys­tem; by be­ing quite struc­tured in ex­plain­ing the rea­sons be­hind our de­ci­sions; and ul­ti­mately, by be­ing a bit thick skinned.

At a ba­sic level, the vi­sual chal­lenges were rel­a­tively straight for­ward: de­vis­ing pat­terns that sur­faced the right in­for­ma­tion at each level, and quickly com­mu­ni­cated things like tim­ing, com­mit­ment and ca­pac­ity.

Re­search, aligned with web stats, sug­gested that there’s lit­tle ‘cross brows­ing’ be­tween the main site and learn­ing plat­form, and while we wanted to in­crease this (par­tic­u­larly in­ward) traf­ﬁc, it did give us some lat­i­tude to fo­cus the de­sign on the plat­form re­la­tion­ship/​hi­er­ar­chy and page ﬂow.

As men­tioned pre­vi­ously, use of im­age be­came quite an im­por­tant part of the page de­sign hi­er­ar­chy, pro­vid­ing a clear dif­fer­en­ti­a­tion be­tween the more con­nec­tive ‘higher or­der’ pages, and the more fo­cused lower or­der ones.

As as­pects of the de­sign be­came more con­crete, we cre­ated a mix­ture of be­hav­iour di­a­grams and pro­to­types to demon­strate fea­tures and in­ter­ac­tions. These were pri­mar­ily use­ful for their com­mu­nica­tive value — for ex­am­ple, as I was re­spon­si­ble for ini­tial markup, there was no need to cre­ate de­tailed rules around re­spon­sive be­hav­iour, but in or­der to smooth some of the sign-off, I cre­ated re­spon­sive be­hav­iour di­a­grams for the menu sys­tem and the pro­mos.

Out­come

In the ﬁrst three months since the launch, user num­bers grew steadily. Com­pared to the old plat­form dur­ing the same pe­riod the pre­vi­ous year, new users in­creased by 25%, with more than half re­turn­ing at a later date. Dwell times dou­bled, and users vis­ited an av­er­age of three pages per ses­sion
