Through­out 2016, I helped them with the de­vel­op­ment of red­burn­to­day, their in­ter­nal por­tal, into a client fac­ing of­fer­ing. The scope of my in­volve­ment was quite wide, across sev­eral ar­eas of fo­cus, from top level prod­uct align­ment, all the way down to vi­sual de­sign craft.

Through a com­bi­na­tion of work­shops with se­nior stake­hold­ers, and one-to-one user re­search, we helped Red­burn de­ter­mine the un­der­ly­ing value of their prod­uct, and then re-struc­tured / re-de­signed to fo­cus on this value.

In par­tic­u­lar we solved in­ter­ac­tion de­sign prob­lems re­lated to search/​browse, and vi­sual de­sign is­sues around the per­ceived qual­ity of the read­ing ex­pe­ri­ence.

‘Sell­ing‘ the pro­ject

This pro­ject came about by evolv­ing an ex­ist­ing re­la­tion­ship, rather than a ‘cold’ sell – an ap­proach that (along with the de­vel­op­ment of a labs of­fer) has been im­por­tant in de­vel­op­ing the de­sign and UX of­fer at Soft­wire.

Soft­wire were orig­i­nally tasked with the build of Re­durn­To­day, im­ple­ment­ing a large and ex­tremely thor­ough de­sign spec by Af­ter the Flood. Ini­tially the site was for launch in a pri­vate al­pha, for staff and a few choice clients, with a plan to then open to the larger client base.

At this point, my in­volve­ment was largely cer­e­mo­nial — at­tend­ing a few meet­ings, point­ing de­vel­op­ers at a few easy wins, and back-ﬁll­ing a cou­ple of ‘holes’ in the de­sign.

Soon af­ter the site launched, we re­ceived some feed­back that it was­n’t be­ing as well re­ceived as our client hoped, and I be­came in­creas­ingly in­volved in the con­ver­sa­tion.

Early stage pro­to­typ­ing

In my view the de­sign had al­ways had a cou­ple of is­sues — the pages were vi­su­ally very com­pet­i­tive, so I sug­gested a few light touch op­tions here; the IA/​nav­i­ga­tion seemed off (the choices of la­bels, some of the hi­er­ar­chy), but I did­n’t have enough do­main or user knowl­edge to make sen­si­ble in­put yet.

In Red­burn’s view, the biggest is­sue they had was with the in­ter­face for ﬁnd­ing pub­li­ca­tions – which had a very clever, but hard to un­der­stand / ma­nip­u­late set of con­trols – so my team put to­gether some ini­tial ideas to make it more us­able (as UX­Pin pro­to­types).

When we tested with users, while they found the new de­signs eas­ier to ma­nip­u­late, it was still clear that they wanted ‘some­thing dif­fer­ent’ — vi­su­ally, in terms of meet­ing the ex­pec­ta­tions set by the printed pub­li­ca­tions; but more fun­da­men­tally in terms of or­gan­i­sa­tion.

We con­tin­ued to it­er­ate on the pro­to­types to help ex­pand our un­der­stand­ing — mov­ing into HTML, and us­ing faker to help gen­er­ate re­al­is­tic data to im­prov­ing our test­ing.

We also per­suded Red­burn to let us con­duct some work­shops and ses­sions to bet­ter un­der­stand the scope of this mis-match and bet­ter ad­dress other is­sues through the site.

Re­search & clar­i­fy­ing pur­pose

We con­ducted sev­eral work­shops with mixed groups, com­pris­ing sales, re­search and ad­min staff from Red­burn along­side a hand­ful of clients.

In these ses­sions we worked with them on a num­ber of ex­er­cises to hone down what they felt was core value of Red­burn to its cus­tomers; and by col­lab­o­ra­tively scor­ing, group­ing and ex­pand­ing on these with par­tic­u­lar fea­tures, we came to a value pri­ori­ti­sa­tion for the por­tal.

It was rel­a­tively hard work (the par­tic­i­pants weren’t nec­ces­sar­ily the most en­gaged with this sort of ac­tiv­ity) yet the work­shops ger­mi­nated very valu­able con­ver­sa­tions, which we able to cap­ture and utilise.

It be­came clear that the thing Red­burn was most val­ued for was a sin­gu­lar, high-qual­ity opin­ion — their clients have lots of sources of data, what they want is to know what spe­ciﬁc thing they should care about “right” and why.

We were then able to re­work the de­sign to re­ﬂect this in­sight — greatly sim­pli­fy­ing the data and mov­ing the fo­cus to the as­pects that ‘Red­burn say you should know’.

We were due to en­gage in fur­ther ses­sions with Red­burn cus­tomers (again a dif­ﬁ­cult thing to con­vince the wider group to let us un­der­take); when some un­re­lated se­cu­rity is­sues with the ex­ist­ing de­vel­op­ment team caused a hia­tus in our work. Un­for­tu­nately when we re­sumed work a few months later, we were un­able to re­visit this.

While I don’t feel that our ap­proach would have al­tered sig­nif­i­cantly, I do think that we lacked in some ‘re­search clout’ to al­low us to ar­gue the case for some de­sign ap­proaches, and which has left the de­sign more open to ‘steer’ from se­nior in­ter­nal staff.

Vi­sual lan­guage — card el­e­ments

Fol­low­ing the work­shops, we worked to re­fo­cus the por­tal to­wards this sim­pler, more ‘opin­ion­at­ed’ ap­proach.

I’d al­ready be­gun to ex­per­i­ment with a card ap­proach (sup­ported with mean­ing­ful, con­tex­tual group­ings) for the pub­li­ca­tion pro­to­types, and these had res­onated well with the small num­ber of users I’d man­aged to talk to. Tak­ing this ap­proach seemed to of­fer a way of com­mu­ni­cat­ing the ‘craft’ and value of the printed doc­u­ments more ef­fec­tively, so I it­er­ated on these, de­vel­op­ing a sys­tem to al­low dif­fer­ing types of in­for­ma­tion to be dis­played in this way.

The site pro­vides users with ‘pub­li­ca­tions’ at a num­ber of lev­els – there are those full of in-depth re­search, which might have taken months to put to­gether, but there are also nu­mer­ous, more im­me­di­ate ones that the sys­tem col­lates, from reg­u­lar daily and weekly up­dates all the way down to emails. In dif­fer­ing ways, these all pro­vide spe­ciﬁc value, so one of the chal­lenges was to de­vise a sys­tem to quickly com­mu­ni­cate these dif­fer­ent types – I worked on a kind of dual logic, where el­e­ment lay­out is ﬂex­i­bly based on data ‘avail­abil­i­ty’, while other vi­sual as­pects was de­rived from the type of doc­u­ment (al­though in prac­tise cer­tain types would pre­clude el­e­ments be­ing avail­able).

This card ap­proach was ex­tended to the other do­main el­e­ments, al­low­ing them to sit in in the same page grids and search re­sults.

For many of el­e­ments, this meant a more com­pli­cated re­spon­sive de­sign, pri­ori­tisng the core in­for­ma­tion, and in­creas­ing dis­play with in­creas­ing size. With­out di­rect ac­cess to users at this point, I re­lied on the in­put of Red­burn staff to help de­ﬁne this.

Ex­pand­ing search pat­tern

Tak­ing these cards, and a more opin­ion­ated fo­cus, and com­bin­ing it with the fed­er­a­tion that we’d al­ready been test­ing with the pub­li­ca­tions pro­to­type — we de­vel­oped a pat­tern for the site wide search, adding a layer of ‘best guess’.

Ini­tially this was just based on some sta­tic pri­ori­tis­ing of types (stocks > sec­tors > pub­li­ca­tions > peo­ple…), com­bined with data ac­tiv­ity (ie a stock that’s just has a ma­jor pub­li­ca­tion will come above one that’s just been made a BUY, which will come above one that’s more dor­mant), long-term the plan is to build more in­tel­li­gence into the sys­tem, so it be­comes more fo­cused to in­di­vid­ual users.

Page struc­ture and IA

As part of the task of sim­pli­fy­ing and fo­cus­ing the data, we im­ple­mented the same sec­tioned page struc­ture to all of the core ‘hub’ pages.

At the same time, I looked at some is­sues with the nav­i­ga­tion / IA.

From the start, I felt that there were some odd choices in the nav­i­ga­tion struc­ture – some strange sep­a­ra­tions of con­tent and con­trol, and some la­bels which seemed to re­ﬂect in­di­vid­ual de­part­ments, rather than re­ﬂect­ing the lan­guage and ac­tions that I’d heard users dis­cuss. For ex­am­ple, to view Tesco, or Sains­bury’s ﬁ­nan­cials, one needed to nav­i­gate via Re­search > Sec­tors > Food Re­tail then to the stock (no-one I tested with guessed Re­search 1st time); but to com­pare them one needed to go via Nerve Cenre > Com­pare and then choose the stocks; where logic would seem to sug­gest the abil­ity to com­pare some­thing from its screen.

Tak­ing things I’d heard, plus ap­ply­ing some fairly sim­ple ap­proaches (la­bels based on things you can point at), I cre­ated some spec­u­la­tive struc­tures, ran some tree­jack tests and fed the re­sults back to Red­burn.

Based on the task im­prove­ments we saw, we im­ple­mented the sim­pler struc­ture and nav­i­ga­tion la­bels.

Read­ing ex­pe­ri­ence

One of the key ﬁnd­ings from our re­search was the per­cep­tion of “miss­ing value” from the on­line pub­li­ca­tions.

Some of this was in­evitable, given the con­tent ve­loc­ity – there’s lit­tle or no op­por­tu­nity to craft an email, ag­gre­gated by the sys­tem (and nor should you – its value lies in its speed); but there are a num­ber of pub­li­ca­tions, which have care and at­ten­tion ap­plied to them, ex­hibit­ing high qual­ity and craft (with a beau­ti­ful de­sign sys­tem, cre­ated by Pen­ta­gram), and for which clients pay a sig­nif­i­cant pre­mium. Yet these were be­ing up­loaded with lit­tle thought given to trans­lat­ing any of that pre­mium into an on­line read­ing ex­pe­ri­ence.

I spent some time with the in­ter­nal team re­spon­si­ble for the printed out­put, to gain their in­sight into the way the struc­ture worked for their clients, and to bet­ter un­der­stand the pro­duc­tion method­ol­ogy in or­der to align with it, then set about ad­dress­ing the de­sign.

Some con­ver­sa­tions, mainly with Red­burn folk (al­though I man­aged to speak to a cou­ple of clients), lead to the thought that it was un­likely that any­one would read a whole re­port on­line (the printed docs of­ten run to 300+ pages, of fairly dense info); and sug­gested that a bet­ter ap­proach would be to pro­vide an en­gag­ing ‘top line’ ex­pe­ri­ence, with clear sign­posts to the deeper con­tent, and a way of pulling to­gether richer con­tent (video) and con­nec­tions (re­lated re­ports, data and par­tic­u­larly on-go­ing con­ver­sa­tion) around the main pub­li­ca­tion.

Be­yond this, as with a lot of ed­i­to­r­ial de­sign, most of the work was in­volved in cre­at­ing some sim­ple rules, and get­ting out of the way of the con­tent. On of the few fea­tures we added was a sticky header, al­low­ing easy ac­cess to top-level con­trols (down­load, share etc) but sig­nif­i­cantly to hold play­ing video – the video con­tent that Red­burn cre­ates tends to be the au­thor’s talk­ing head overview, some­thing for which fullscreen play­back is less use­ful than the abil­ity to read and lis­ten.

Im­pact

To date, we’ve not had chance to get any mean­ing­ful quant data – the prod­uct is only avail­able to a se­lect few of Red­burn’s clients.
